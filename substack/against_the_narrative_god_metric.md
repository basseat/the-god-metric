# The God Metric: Everyone Says Religion Is Dying. Most of the World Didn't Get That Memo.

*Against the Narrative — No. 3*

---

There is a story that educated people in London, New York, and Berlin tend to agree on: religion is in decline. Science is winning. The secular tide is rising. Give it a generation and the God question will sort itself out.

I believed some version of this. Then I spent a few months building a database to test it.

What I found was not that the story is completely wrong. It is that the story is about two places, dressed up as a story about the whole world.

---

Here is what nobody tells you about secularisation: the fastest decline in religious affiliation right now is not in Western Europe. It is in North America.

That surprised me too. You hear about the empty cathedrals in England, the closing churches in France, Christianity slowly retreating from the continent where it dominated for a thousand years. That story is real. But the data from 2010 to 2020 shows Americans and Canadians walking away from religion at three times the rate of Europeans. The "rise of the nones" — people ticking "no religion" on surveys — has been the real action of the last twenty years, and it has been happening in the US more than anywhere.

Even so, zoom out to the whole world and neither the American trend nor the European one tells you much. Sub-Saharan Africa averages 95% religious affiliation and is flat. South Asia is at 99%, flat. The Middle East is at 99%, flat. Latin America sits around 90% with barely a dent over the decade. We are talking about the majority of the world's population in these regions, and the secular tide has simply not arrived there. In most of them, it shows no sign of arriving at all.

The secularisation story is true in the places where most of its storytellers live. Applied to the whole planet, it describes a minority of the world and mistakes it for the whole.

---

Now here is the one that really got me.

In 1910, Sub-Saharan Africa had approximately 7 million Christians. Seven million. Europe had 400 million, representing about 72% of all the Christians on earth. Christianity, in 1910, was genuinely and overwhelmingly a European religion.

By 2010, Sub-Saharan Africa had 516 million Christians. Europe had 553 million.

Those two numbers are almost the same. And they will not be almost the same for much longer. By 2050, Pew projects Sub-Saharan Africa will hold around 1.1 billion Christians, roughly 40% of the entire global total. Europe is projected at under 17%.

The centre of gravity of the world's largest religion moved from Europe to Africa over the course of a single century, and most people in Europe have no idea this happened. The picture they carry in their heads is of Christianity as a Western thing, a European inheritance, something their grandparents practised more than they do. That picture is a hundred years out of date.

The largest Christian populations on earth are in Sub-Saharan Africa and Latin America. The fastest-growing churches are in Nigeria, Kenya, Ghana, and Brazil. The crossover, the moment Africa overtakes Europe as the single biggest Christian bloc, is happening right now, if it hasn't already.

---

The third thing I looked at was whether mobile phones and the internet were speeding up secularisation, particularly in Africa. The logic seems obvious: people get smartphones, they get access to the wider world, they drift away from traditional religion. That is certainly what happened in parts of Europe.

In Africa, that is not what happened.

Nigeria went from almost no mobile phone penetration in 1999 to over 70 phones per 100 people by 2010. Over that exact same decade, Pentecostal Christianity exploded across the country. The same pattern shows up in Kenya and Ghana. More phones, more religion.

Why? Probably because mobile technology does not only expose people to secular ideas. It also lets religious communities do things they could never do before: broadcast sermons to millions, organise WhatsApp groups by church, build audiences that no local congregation could ever reach. The technology is neutral. In Africa, the dominant social force it ended up amplifying was faith, not doubt.

---

So what is going on? Why does the secular-tide story survive when the data tells a different one?

Part of it is geography. The people who write about religion tend to live in the places where religion is declining. A sociologist in Manchester observing empty pews in the 1970s was not wrong about Manchester. The mistake was calling Manchester a preview of the whole world.

Part of it is what gets covered. A closing church in England is a news story with a photograph. A new Pentecostal congregation opening in Lagos — one of hundreds that week — never makes the news at all. What gets reported shapes what people believe is happening.

The result is a story that feels like a global truth and is actually a regional one. Religion is not dying. It is moving. The geography of faith is shifting south, the numbers involved are enormous, and the picture most people have in their heads is not where the world actually is.
